## **The Day the AI Industry Split: Anthropic Banned, OpenAI Enlisted, and the Safety Governance Experiment Ends**

If yesterday's edition was about a safety floor in motion, today's edition is about a floor that just gave way entirely.

In the past 18 hours, the AI safety governance experiment — the idea that responsible AI labs could voluntarily negotiate guardrails with state actors — collapsed in real time. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth declared Anthropic a "supply-chain risk to national security" and banned every federal contractor from doing business with the company. Hours later, Sam Altman announced OpenAI would deploy its models on classified DoD networks with no such restrictions. The split is now structural: one lab held its line on autonomous weapons and mass surveillance use cases, got blacklisted for it, and another took the contract.

For security practitioners, the implications are not theoretical. The AI tooling that underpins a growing fraction of enterprise security operations — threat triage, code scanning, SOC automation — now exists in a regulatory and political environment where the companies building those tools are being compelled to choose sides. The question of whether your AI vendor has agreed to allow its model to operate autonomous weapons is no longer a policy abstraction. It's a vendor risk management question.

This is Saturday's edition. Here is what happened overnight.

## **Breaking News & Phase Shifts**

### **1. Hegseth Bans Anthropic from All DoD Contractor Work — Anthropic Vows to Sue**

The standoff that built through the week resolved in the worst possible way for AI safety governance. At approximately 10 PM ET on Friday, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth posted on X:

> *"Effective immediately, no contractor, supplier, or partner that does business with the United States military may conduct any commercial activity with Anthropic. America's warfighters will never be held hostage by the ideological whims of Big Tech. This decision is final."*

President Trump followed with an executive order requiring all federal agencies to "immediately" cease using Anthropic services, with a six-month transition window for DoD and certain other agencies.

**What the fight was actually about:** The Guardian and NPR's reporting clarifies what the Pentagon wanted that Anthropic refused to give:
1. **Removal of guardrails prohibiting Claude's use in mass domestic surveillance of Americans**
2. **Authorization for Claude's deployment in fully autonomous weapons systems** — meaning weapons that fire without human-in-the-loop authorization

Anthropic's statement called the supply chain designation "legally unsound" and a "dangerous precedent for any American company that negotiates with the government," noting that Hegseth lacks statutory authority to extend a DoD supply-chain risk designation to bar contractors from independent commercial relationships with a vendor.

**The pivot that makes this a watershed:** Within hours of the Anthropic ban, Sam Altman posted that OpenAI had agreed to deploy its models in classified DoD networks. The message was unambiguous — OpenAI is taking the contract, with presumably fewer use-case restrictions than Anthropic insisted on. The HN community's reaction was swift: within 10 hours, the Anthropic statement had 2,876 upvotes and 1,543 comments. A top HN post: "How do I cancel my ChatGPT subscription?" — at 921 points. That's a protest vote in upvote form.

**What this means for enterprise security:** Defense contractor supply chains will need to document and replace any Anthropic-based tooling within six months. Security teams inside those orgs need to treat this as a procurement incident: inventory your Claude API usage, assess replacement timelines, and evaluate whether OpenAI or alternative providers cover your use cases. More broadly, any enterprise evaluating AI security tooling should now add "government use-case restrictions" to vendor questionnaires — this split demonstrates that vendor policy choices have real operational consequences.

### **2. Veracode 2026 SOSS: The AI Velocity Trap — More Bugs Created Than Fixed**

Veracode published its 2026 State of Software Security report this week, and the headline finding is one that deserves to live in every CISO deck for the rest of the year: **the pace of flaw creation now outpaces the capacity to fix them.**

The data, drawn from 1.6 million applications tested on Veracode's cloud platform:
- **82% of organizations carry active security debt** (up from 74% in 2025)
- **60% carry critical security debt** (up from 50% in 2025)
- AI-assisted development velocity is the named driver: organizations using AI coding tools are shipping code faster than their security review cycles can absorb

The Register's framing: *"High-velocity development with AI is making comprehensive security unattainable."*

**The structural problem this identifies:** AI coding tools (Copilot, Claude Code, Cursor) are dramatically accelerating the rate at which code enters production. They are not dramatically accelerating the rate at which vulnerabilities in that code are identified and remediated. The delta between those two rates is compounding security debt faster than most organizations' existing AppSec programs were designed to handle.

**The Claude Code Security angle:** Anthropic's February 20 launch of Claude Code Security — a codebase vulnerability scanner — sent cybersecurity stocks down as much as 11% on fears it would disintermediate AppSec vendors. The iShares Expanded Tech Software Sector ETF is down 26% in 2026. But the Veracode data suggests the actual problem is that AI is creating vulnerabilities faster than any scanner can flag them, not that scanning capacity is the bottleneck.

> **The practitioner read-through:** If you're evaluating AI coding tools for developer productivity, that evaluation needs a parallel AppSec velocity assessment. What is your current mean-time-to-fix on discovered vulnerabilities? What happens to that metric if your code production rate doubles? Veracode's data says 82% of you already can't keep up. AI coding tools are a fuel-to-fire scenario without a parallel investment in AI-assisted remediation.

### **3. 2,800 Exposed Google API Keys Gain Dangerous Gemini Privileges — Silently**

TruffleSecurity published research this week documenting a threat category hiding in plain sight: **legacy Google API keys previously considered non-sensitive now silently authenticate to Gemini AI endpoints**, exposing private data and enabling attackers to run costly API calls at victims' expense.

The mechanism: When Google introduced Gemini, developers enabled the Gemini API alongside existing Google Cloud projects. Google Cloud API keys — freely embedded in client-side JavaScript for years to enable Maps, YouTube embeds, and Firebase services without security concern — now also authenticate to Gemini. Nobody warned the developers whose keys were already public.

**The scale:** TruffleSecurity scanned the November 2025 Common Crawl dataset and found **2,800+ live Google API keys publicly exposed** in page source code. Affected organizations include major financial institutions, security companies, and Google itself.

**The attack surface:**
- An attacker copies an API key from any public website's JavaScript
- Uses it to access private Gemini conversation history and document data
- Alternatively, maxes out API calls generating **thousands of dollars per day in charges** billed to the victim

Google classified the issue as "single-service privilege escalation" after a November 21, 2025 disclosure but has not rotated or invalidated the exposed keys.

**Operational guidance:** Run TruffleSecurity's scanner or equivalent secret scanning against your public-facing JavaScript now. Any Google API key in client-side code should be treated as potentially Gemini-scoped. Rotate everything. Apply API key restrictions in the Google Cloud Console — restrict by HTTP referrer, IP, and restrict Gemini API access explicitly.

## **Technical Deep Dives**

### **1. Agentic AI as Threat Multiplier: The Autonomous Attacker Doesn't Stop**

Barracuda Networks published a detailed threat analysis yesterday that belongs in the same category as the Amazon FortiGate case study from earlier this week: it documents not just that agentic AI is being weaponized, but *how* the autonomous attacker operates differently from its human-controlled predecessor.

The critical distinction: where generative AI gives attackers better tools (faster phishing, better malware), **agentic AI gives them a collaborative partner that can plan, adapt, and persist without returning to a human operator between steps**.

Practical differences for defenders:

| Generative AI Attacker | Agentic AI Attacker |
|---|---|
| Requires human at each decision point | Autonomous retry and adaptation |
| Stops when blocked | Finds alternative paths and continues |
| Human-paced execution | Machine-speed multi-stage execution |
| Static attack plan | Dynamic replanning based on environment |

Ransomware groups named in the analysis — Qilin, Cl0p, DireWolf, The Gentlemen — are now moving at agentic speeds. **Your incident response playbooks were designed for human-paced attackers.** An agentic adversary that doesn't stop after a failed attempt invalidates any playbook that assumes attacker hesitation or regrouping.

The three mitigations that still work against agentic attackers:
1. **Strong identity controls** — agents can't pivot without valid credentials
2. **Network segmentation** — lateral movement requires traversable paths
3. **Behavior-based detection** — agentic patterns look different from human patterns, but your tools need tuning

### **2. MIT CSAIL: 81% of AI Agents Have No Kill Switch, 70% Disclose Nothing About Safety Testing**

MIT CSAIL surveyed 30 of the most widely deployed agentic AI systems and produced findings that should be mandatory reading before any enterprise agentic deployment:

- **Only 19% of deployed agent systems disclose formal safety policies**
- **70% disclose nothing about security testing** — if any was conducted
- **Most have no documented mechanism to shut down a rogue agent**

The meta-irony documented by ZDNET: OpenAI hiring Peter Steinberg (creator of OpenClaw) is driving agentic technology "fully into the mainstream" while OpenClaw itself was recently documented to have dramatic security flaws including the ability to "completely hijack your personal computer." An agent deployed to improve organizational security may itself represent an undisclosed attack surface.

The SF Standard captured the archetype this week: *"Meta's AI safety director lost control of her agent. It started deleting her emails."*

**Five-question vendor evaluation checklist for any agentic AI deployment:**
1. Does the vendor publish a formal safety policy?
2. Has independent third-party security testing been conducted and disclosed?
3. Is there a documented kill switch / emergency shutdown procedure?
4. What are the agent's blast radius limits — what systems can it access, what actions can it take without approval?
5. Is there an immutable audit log of all agent actions?

If vendors can't answer these five questions, they haven't done the baseline work.

### **3. "God-Like" Attack Machines: AI Agents Bypass Security Policies by Design**

Dark Reading published research this week cutting to the architectural root of the agentic security problem. The finding: *"Any system that relies on guardrails to prevent AI agents from interacting with resources beyond their permission scope is vulnerable by design."*

The specific failure mode is **permission scope drift** — agents granted access to system A in order to complete task B inevitably encounter paths to system C that were not intended to be in scope, and current guardrail architectures have no reliable mechanism to enforce those boundaries at runtime.

The offensive flip: Elastic Security Labs published this week on agentic AI SOCs that correlate multi-modal telemetry, prioritize complete attack chains over isolated alerts, and execute containment at machine speed. The same architecture that makes agents dangerous as attackers makes them powerful as defenders. The differentiator is governance, not the underlying technology.

The practical read: the mitigation for agentic boundary drift is not better guardrails. It's **identity-based access, least privilege, environment isolation, continuous monitoring, and audit logs** — the same controls that were good practice before agents existed. Agents don't create new security primitives; they amplify the consequences of getting the basics wrong.
