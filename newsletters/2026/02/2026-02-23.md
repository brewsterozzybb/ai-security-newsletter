## **The 72-Minute Clock: AI Has Compressed the Attack Lifecycle to a Single Work Meeting**

Two major reports dropped this week — from Palo Alto Networks' Unit 42 and Cisco — and read side by side, they describe the same fundamental shift from different angles. The attacker's clock is now 72 minutes. The defender's security architecture was built for 285.

The **2026 Unit 42 Global Incident Response Report**, based on 750+ real-world incidents, found that the median time-to-exfiltration has collapsed from 285 minutes (2024) to just **72 minutes** in 2025 — a 4x acceleration driven almost entirely by AI as a "force multiplier." Attackers are using AI to automate reconnaissance and exploit vulnerabilities within minutes of public disclosure, compress the phishing-to-credential-theft pipeline, and navigate lateral movement inside environments faster than human analysts can even be paged.

The pattern that emerges is stark: **90% of incidents involved identity weaknesses as a material factor.** Not zero-days. Not novel exploits. Attackers are logging in. They're using stolen credentials, hijacked sessions, and — increasingly — an organization's own internal AI services to map systems and escalate access once inside. The 99% of cloud identities holding excessive permissions isn't a misconfiguration problem anymore; it's the dominant attack surface.

> **"AI is a force multiplier for attackers, allowing them to automate reconnaissance and exploit vulnerabilities in minutes. The window to stop an attack has collapsed to 72 minutes — faster than most security teams can even triage an alert."** — 2026 Unit 42 Global Incident Response Report, Palo Alto Networks

The Cisco **State of AI Security 2026** report, published Thursday, describes the same acceleration from the model side. Multi-turn jailbreak attacks — those that unfold across extended conversations rather than in a single prompt — achieved **success rates as high as 92%** in testing across eight open-weight models. The finding is particularly dangerous for agentic deployments: single-turn protections, the kind most teams have implemented, provide "less assurance during longer sessions that involved memory and tool access." An agent running a two-hour autonomous workflow has a fundamentally different jailbreak exposure than a chatbot answering one question.

Cisco AI Threat Intelligence Lead Amy Chang put the implication directly: **"Multi-turn resilience should be tracked as a separate metric, especially for agents that operate over longer sessions."** Most enterprises are not tracking this at all.

### **The Compounding Problem: 29% Prepared, 100% Deployed**

The readiness gap is the number that should be on every CISO's slides this week. According to Cisco's data, most organizations planned to deploy agentic AI into business functions — and **only 29% reported that they were prepared to secure those deployments**. That gap created exposure across model interfaces, tool integrations, and supply chains that is already being exploited.

**Immediate priorities:**
- Instrument agentic AI sessions for multi-turn behavioral monitoring — not just single-prompt filters
- Treat 72 minutes as your maximum mean-time-to-detect target for identity anomalies
- Audit cloud IAM posture: 99% over-permissioned identities is not a hypothetical; it's your current state

## **FortiGate Follow-Through: The Story Gets Bigger as New Details Surface**

The AWS/FortiGate disclosure — first surfaced Friday and covered in-depth in Saturday's edition — is getting a second wave of coverage this morning as **The Register** publishes a detailed technical analysis and additional context emerges.

The Register's Monday report adds texture that was missing from the initial disclosure: the actor's use of AI wasn't incidental — it was structurally load-bearing. The AI tools handled operational planning, tool development, command generation, and automated lateral movement. Without them, the campaign simply wouldn't have been executable at this scale by an actor of this capability level.

What's drawing sustained attention in the security community today is not just the campaign itself but the **precedent it sets for disclosure**. Amazon's decision to publish attack telemetry — including code analysis that fingerprints AI-generated tooling — is being treated as a model for how cloud providers should communicate. The code signatures (redundant comments that restate function names, string-matched JSON parsing, naive architecture) are now circulating as blue-team detection artifacts for post-compromise forensics.

## **UAE Foils AI-Powered "Terrorist Cyberattacks" on Vital Infrastructure — A National Defense First**

Over the weekend, the UAE Cybersecurity Council announced the successful thwarting of a series of **coordinated AI-powered attacks against national digital infrastructure** — what they characterized as "organised cyber attacks of a terrorist nature."

The UAE statement is notable for its specificity: the attacks included **network infiltration attempts, ransomware deployment, and systematic phishing campaigns** against national platforms. Crucially, the Council explicitly cited **"exploitation of artificial intelligence technologies to develop sophisticated offensive tools"** — marking this as one of the first official government statements to attribute a nation-state-adjacent attack campaign directly to AI-augmented offensive tooling.

The head of the UAE Cybersecurity Council, Dr. Mohamed Al Kuwaiti, had warned just days earlier that the country faces **90,000 to 200,000 breach attempts every day**, many state-sponsored. He attributed rising attack frequency to "regional geopolitical tension" and specifically called out **AI-enabled disinformation activity** increasing hacktivist mobilization across regional digital ecosystems.

Asia accounts for 66.7% of attributed state-sponsored actor origins, with Europe at 14.3% and Middle East/cross-regional actors making up the remainder — a distribution that suggests the Gulf's increasingly prominent role in global AI infrastructure makes it a high-value target for multiple threat actor communities.

> **Why it matters beyond the Middle East:** If AI-augmented attack tooling is now appearing in "terrorist" designations and government incident disclosures at the national defense level, we are past the point where this is a cybersecurity problem. This is geopolitical infrastructure risk.

## **Technical Deep Dives**

### **1. SANDWORM_MODE: The npm Worm That Targets AI Toolchains Is Worse Than Reported**

A supply chain worm campaign dubbed **SANDWORM_MODE** by Socket security researchers has expanded to 19 malicious npm packages — and its most technically significant component isn't the credential harvesting. It's the **McpInject module**, which specifically targets AI coding assistants by deploying a malicious MCP server into tool configurations on disk.

Here's what McpInject does, precisely:
1. **Deploys a rogue MCP server** masquerading as a legitimate tool provider into the configuration files of Claude Code, Claude Desktop, Cursor, VS Code Continue, and Windsurf
2. **Registers three seemingly benign tools**, each of which embeds hidden prompt injection instructions
3. **Reads sensitive files** — `~/.ssh/id_rsa`, `~/.ssh/id_ed25519`, `~/.aws/credentials`, `~/.npmrc`, `.env` — and stages them for exfiltration
4. **Harvests LLM API keys** for nine AI providers

The worm goes further: it contains a weaponized GitHub Action that harvests CI/CD secrets with DNS fallback exfiltration, and a destructive "kill switch" that wipes the home directory if GitHub/npm access is lost (currently disabled by default). The packages propagate by abusing stolen npm and GitHub identities.

The typosquat package names are precise — `claud-code`, `cloude-code`, `cloude` — targeting developers who would install what they believe is Claude Code. The campaign follows the Cline supply chain attack from February 17; the pattern of AI toolchain packages as high-value targets is now a confirmed, repeated campaign vector.

> **The architectural insight this reveals:** MCP server injection is a persistent, low-noise attack vector. Unlike a process injection or network beacon, a rogue MCP server entry in a config file can sit undetected for weeks. No existing SIEM rule detects this. It requires configuration-file integrity monitoring specifically for AI tool config directories.

**Detection artifacts:**
- Check `~/.config/claude/`, `~/.cursor/`, `~/.vscode/continue/` for unexpected MCP server entries
- Audit MCP tool registries for unknown publishers
- Monitor outbound DNS for patterns consistent with C2 fallback

### **2. MuddyWater's Operation Olalampo: Iranian APT Deploys AI-Assisted Toolchain Against MENA**

Iran-nexus APT group MuddyWater (tracked also as Earth Vetala, Mango Sandstorm) has launched a new campaign — **Operation Olalampo** — targeting organizations across the Middle East and North Africa, active since January 26, 2026, according to a Group-IB disclosure published over the weekend.

The campaign deploys four new malware families with overlapping samples previously attributed to MuddyWater:

- **GhostFetch** — A first-stage downloader with sophisticated anti-analysis: validates mouse movements, checks screen resolution, detects debuggers and VM artifacts, and fetches secondary payloads directly in memory (bypassing disk-based AV)
- **GhostBackDoor** — A second-stage implant delivered by GhostFetch supporting interactive shell, file read/write, and GhostFetch re-execution
- **HTTP_VIP** — A native downloader that conducts system reconnaissance, connects to C2 (`codefusiontech[.]org`), and deploys AnyDesk for persistent remote access. Updated variant adds clipboard capture and dynamic beaconing intervals
- **CHAR** — A Rust-written backdoor controlled by a **Telegram bot** (username: `stager_51_bot`, display name: "Olalampo") — using Telegram's API as C2 infrastructure to execute shell commands and PowerShell

The infection chain starts with phishing emails carrying malicious Microsoft Excel documents that prompt macro enablement. Lures include flight ticket themes and reports, as well as impersonation of an energy and marine services company.

**Security Implications:** GhostFetch's in-memory execution bypasses most endpoint solutions relying on disk-write detection. The Telegram-as-C2 pattern is increasingly common among Iranian actors — the traffic blends with legitimate Telegram usage, and most enterprise proxies permit it. **Organizations in MENA, energy, and maritime sectors should treat `codefusiontech[.]org` as a hard IOC and review Telegram API traffic from non-browser processes.**

The timing is notable given the UAE's weekend disclosure of AI-augmented attacks — these may be separate campaigns or related pressure across the same geopolitical theater.

## **Enterprise Crisis & Governance**

### **Google's GTIG AI Threat Tracker: "Model Extraction Is IP Theft at Scale"**

Google's Threat Intelligence Group (GTIG) published its latest **AI Threat Tracker** last week — and the Monday morning read is sobering. Chief analyst John Hultquist documents five categories of adversarial AI misuse that have moved from theoretical to observed in the last quarter of 2025:

1. **Model extraction attacks** — Adversaries using knowledge distillation to extract training information from frontier models and transfer it to models they control. Google observed over **100,000 model extraction attempts** in Q4 2025 alone. The IP theft is a concrete business risk. Hultquist's explicit warning: *organizations that expose AI models via API should monitor for extraction and distillation patterns now*, before this capability diffuses from frontier labs to commodity attackers.

2. **AI-augmented agentic reconnaissance** — China-nexus group **APT31** has been experimenting with agentic capabilities to automate reconnaissance and scale operations. This is the first GTIG attribution of agentic AI use to a specific named threat actor.

3. **Dynamic social engineering** — North Korean and Iranian actors have evolved from using AI as a template tool to using it as a **dynamic interaction engine** that can develop social engineering in real time, including fake recruiter personas that conduct complete interview processes to deliver malware.

4. **AI-enabled disinformation** — State actors using AI to scale narrative manipulation in parallel with technical intrusion campaigns.

5. **Infrastructure targeting via AI-optimized exploit development** — Faster vulnerability identification and PoC generation, directly compressing the patch window.

> **The macro signal from GTIG:** "Adversaries are experimenting, distilling, and beginning to integrate AI into their operations. The future has already arrived." — John Hultquist, Chief Analyst, Google Threat Intelligence Group

### **Cisco Warns of the "SolarWinds of AI" — And Describes Exactly How It Happens**

The most consequential governance framing in Cisco's State of AI Security 2026 report isn't the jailbreak statistics — it's the supply chain warning. Cisco is explicitly raising the scenario of **a coordinated mass supply-chain attack where a widely used AI library or foundation model is compromised at the source** — what they're calling the potential "SolarWinds of AI."

The mechanism is already demonstrated at smaller scale. Cisco documented a case where an attacker published a malicious npm package designed to mimic an MCP integration for the Postmark email platform. The package **blind-carbon-copied every email sent through the agent to an attacker-controlled address** — silently. No alarms. No failed requests. Just an invisible copy of every invoice, password reset, and internal memo flowing to an adversary.

The implication for enterprise AI governance is precise: **MCP servers, agent tool registries, and context brokers need to be governed with the same hardened approach as API gateways and databases.** They are not developer tooling. They are trust boundaries. Today, they are largely unmonitored.

Cisco's structural recommendation: apply least-privilege APIs to AI model interactions and deploy behavioral monitoring on agent activity — not just access control checks at connection time.
