## **The Safety Floor Just Dropped: Anthropic Removes Its Hard Stop on Unsafe Model Deployment**

Two stories dropped in the same 24-hour window this week, and read together they are the clearest signal yet of how quickly the AI safety governance environment is deteriorating under competitive and military pressure.

**Story One:** The Pentagon — specifically Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth — reportedly gave Anthropic a deadline to yield its AI safeguards and provide the U.S. military with unrestrained access to Claude for any application it chooses, including offensive operations. The pressure campaign had been building for weeks.

**Story Two:** On the same day that Hegseth pressure became public, Anthropic quietly revised its **Responsible Scaling Policy (RSP)** — the document that, since the company's founding, had served as its central safety commitment. The previous RSP included an explicit hard tripwire: Anthropic would **pause scaling and delay deployment** of any model that outpaced its own safety evaluation capabilities. The updated RSP removes that clause entirely.

Anthropic's Chief Science Officer Jared Kaplan framed the change in terms that are hard to argue with in a vacuum: *"We felt that it wouldn't actually help anyone for us to stop training AI models. We didn't really feel, with the rapid advance of AI, that it made sense for us to make unilateral commitments… if competitors are blazing ahead."*

The timing — same day as the DoD pressure story — is what practitioners are circling. Anthropic has been emphatic that the changes are coincidental. The security community is skeptical.

### **Why This Matters Beyond the Headlines**

The RSP was not just marketing. It was the structural backbone of Anthropic's pitch to enterprise customers and regulators: the company would refuse to deploy models it couldn't guarantee were safe. That hard limit was the distinguishing feature that allowed Anthropic to credibly claim safety differentiation from OpenAI and Google. **Its removal is a material change to the trust model that many enterprises built their AI procurement decisions around.**

For security practitioners, the downstream implications are concrete:
- **Military AI use cases** that previously required safety reviews may proceed without them under the updated policy
- **The precedent is now set** — if Anthropic, the industry's most safety-positioned lab, can remove its hard limits under competitive pressure, the expectation that any lab will hold lines under comparable pressure is weakened
- **NIST's AI Agent Standards RFI** (comment deadline March 9) takes on new urgency: voluntary commitments from labs are clearly insufficient; standards bodies and regulators are now the only credible governance mechanism

Anthropic also closed a **$30 billion Series G** this month. The company is at a strategic inflection point where commercial scale and safety absolutism are in direct tension, and the RSP revision is the first public indication of which side is winning.

> **The read-through:** Last week, Anthropic publicly accused China-based labs of conducting 16-million-query distillation attacks to strip Claude's safety guardrails. This week, Anthropic modified its own policy to weaken the hard limits on deploying models whose safety properties it cannot guarantee. Both directions of travel — external safety stripping and internal policy relaxation — are moving the same way. The frontier AI safety floor is in motion.

## **600 FortiGates in 55 Countries — What a Financially Motivated Amateur With GenAI Access Can Now Do**

Amazon Threat Intelligence published a landmark case study this week that belongs in every board-level AI risk briefing before the end of the month. Between **January 11 and February 18, 2026**, a Russian-speaking threat actor — assessed as **financially motivated, no APT affiliation, limited technical capability** — compromised **over 600 FortiGate devices across 55 countries**.

The actor succeeded not through zero-days, not through sophisticated exploitation, not through FANCY BEAR-level tradecraft. They succeeded by **using commercial generative AI tools to bridge their own skill gap at industrial scale**.

From CJ Moses, CISO of Amazon Integrated Security:

> **"No exploitation of FortiGate vulnerabilities was observed — instead, this campaign succeeded by exploiting exposed management ports and weak credentials with single-factor authentication, fundamental security gaps that AI helped an unsophisticated actor exploit at scale."**

The anatomy of the operation, documented by Amazon, reads as an AI-powered assembly line:
- One primary commercial GenAI tool served as the operational backbone for tool development, attack planning, and command generation
- A second GenAI tool served as fallback for pivot assistance in specific compromised networks
- The actor ran **systematic scanning** across ports 443, 8443, 10443, and 4443 targeting internet-exposed FortiGate management interfaces
- Followed by automated credential-stuffing using commonly reused default credentials
- Upon successful breach: Active Directory compromise, **complete credential database extraction**, targeting of backup infrastructure (pre-ransomware positioning)
- When targets proved hardened, the actor abandoned them immediately and moved to softer victims — AI-assisted triage, not human patience

Amazon's investigators found the actor's publicly accessible infrastructure, which contained AI-generated attack plans, victim configurations, and custom tooling source code.

### **Security Implications**

This is not the first AI-augmented attack. But it is the most clearly documented case of **AI capability democratization producing nation-state-level operational scale from a non-state actor**. The CrowdStrike 2026 GTR documented FANCY BEAR deploying LAMEHUG; that's a state-sponsored actor. This week's FortiGate campaign documents a financially motivated individual or small group achieving comparable scope with commodity GenAI access.

The finding maps directly to X-Force's 2026 data (published Tuesday): the **44% increase in attacks against public-facing applications** isn't coming from increasingly sophisticated adversaries. It's coming from less sophisticated adversaries with increasingly powerful tools.

**The three things that would have stopped this entire campaign:**
1. FortiGate management interfaces not exposed to the internet (access control)
2. Non-default credentials with MFA enabled (credential hygiene)
3. Network segmentation preventing Active Directory access from FortiGate context (defense in depth)

None of these are exotic. All of them are 2015 security advice. The novelty is that **AI is now the equalizer that makes the consequences of failing to apply 2015 advice equivalent to the consequences of failing to stop a nation-state team.**

## **Technical Deep Dives**

### **1. CVE-2026-1731 (CVSS 9.9): BeyondTrust's Pre-Auth RCE Is Dropping Backdoors Across Finance, Healthcare, and Law**

The BeyondTrust vulnerability story has escalated from "actively exploited" to "fully weaponized across critical sectors." Unit 42 published its most detailed findings this week, and the post-exploitation activity documented is comprehensive enough to treat this as a confirmed ransomware precursor campaign.

**The vulnerability:** CVE-2026-1731 is a **pre-authentication remote code execution** flaw in BeyondTrust's Remote Support and Privileged Remote Access (PRA) products, CVSS v4 score 9.9. The flaw lives in the `thin-scc-wrapper` script, which handles incoming WebSocket connections. The mechanism is a bash arithmetic injection: the script uses `(( ... ))` or `let` contexts to compare a client-supplied `remoteVersion` value, and bash arithmetic contexts are not sandboxed — they execute arbitrary commands embedded in the input.

**The result:** An unauthenticated attacker who can reach the WebSocket interface on a BeyondTrust deployment can execute OS commands with high privileges. No account. No interaction. Network exposure plus this bug equals immediate system compromise.

The exploit was **publicly available on the same day the CVE was disclosed (February 10)**. CISA added it to the KEV catalog on February 13.

**What Unit 42 is actually seeing in the wild:**
- Network reconnaissance and new account creation post-breach
- PHP web shell deployment (the documented shell uses `eval()` on POST-submitted code, conditioned on a GET parameter — a compact, hard-to-detect persistent backdoor)
- VShell and SparkRAT command-and-control framework deployment
- Lateral movement including AD enumeration and credential harvesting
- Data exfiltration and backup infrastructure targeting

The confirmed sector victims span **financial services, legal, high technology, higher education, healthcare, and wholesale/retail** in the US, France, Germany, Australia, and Canada. Cortex Xpanse's scan data shows **16,400+ instances still potentially exposed**.

**For any organization running BeyondTrust Remote Support or PRA:** This is a patch-now-verify-later situation. If you haven't already patched to the versions specified in BeyondTrust's February 2026 advisory, you may already have implants. Treat this as an assumed-breach scenario and investigate before patching — web shells survive patches.

### **2. Pseudonymity Is Dead: arXiv Paper Demonstrates LLM-Powered Mass Deanonymization at 68% Recall**

Published this week on arXiv (2602.16800) and rapidly reaching the top of Hacker News, a new paper from researchers Simon Lermen and colleagues delivers a finding that has been theoretically anticipated but never rigorously demonstrated at scale: **LLMs can deanonymize pseudonymous internet users across platforms with 68% recall at 90% precision — compared to near 0% for the best non-LLM method.**

The attack framework, demonstrated across Hacker News, Reddit, LinkedIn, and anonymized interview transcripts (the Anthropic Interviewer dataset), works through a division of labor that makes API-level abuse detection extremely difficult:

1. **Profile summarization** — infer location, profession, interests, behavioral patterns from anonymous posts
2. **Embedding computation** — generate semantic profile vectors
3. **Cross-platform candidate ranking** — identify likely real-world identities from the inferred attributes

Each step individually looks like normal API usage. The compound effect is deanonymization of users who have spent years carefully maintaining separate online identities.

> **"The average online user has long operated under an implicit threat model where they have assumed pseudonymity provides adequate protection because targeted deanonymization would require extensive effort. LLMs invalidate this assumption."** — arXiv:2602.16800

**The attack vector is not theoretical.** The researchers performed real-world validation against the Anthropic Interviewer dataset with manual verification. The practical obscurity that protected pseudonymous identities — the assumption that targeted deanonymization requires skilled human investigators with significant time investment — is gone. The cost of identifying who wrote an anonymous Reddit post about their employer, their medical condition, or their political views has collapsed to near zero.

**Security and compliance implications are immediate:**
- Whistleblower protection programs built on pseudonymous platforms are now significantly weaker than their designers assumed
- Insider threat programs that monitor anonymous forums for employee disclosures face a two-sided problem: the same tools used defensively can be weaponized against employees
- Privacy impact assessments that assumed pseudonymous data as non-personally-identifiable need updating — LLM-enabled inference collapses that category

The researchers propose platform-side mitigations (limiting cross-platform data linkage, rate-limiting inference APIs) and individual mitigations (deliberate style variation across platforms, compartmentalized interests). Neither is a complete solution. The underlying capability is now deployed in commodity form.

## **Enterprise Crisis & Governance**

### **40,000 Exposed OpenClaw Instances, 12,800 RCE-Exploitable — The Shadow AI Sprawl Problem Arrives at Scale**

SecurityScorecard published research this week that crystallizes the shadow AI sprawl problem better than any prior report: **40,214 publicly accessible instances of OpenClaw** (the autonomous AI agent formerly known as Clawdbot/Moltbot), associated with 28,663 unique IP addresses and still growing. Of those, **63% are vulnerable — and 12,812 are exploitable via remote code execution right now**.

The numbers are alarming enough. The composition is worse. SecurityScorecard correlated:
- **549 exposed instances** with prior breach activity
- **1,493 instances** with known vulnerabilities in their observable software stack

OpenClaw instances by design have broad access to the host systems they run on: shell execution, filesystem access, API integrations, email/calendar access, and connected cloud services. A compromised instance doesn't just expose a user's AI conversations. It exposes **every service the agent is authorized to access** — which, in the cases being documented, includes Slack workspaces, Gmail, Google Drive, and cloud API credentials.

The secondary exposure is equally significant: some OpenClaw deployments are leaking **API keys linked to third-party services via their control panels**, further amplifying the value of a successful compromise.

Jeremy Turner, VP of Threat Intelligence at SecurityScorecard:

> **"The more centralized the access, the more damage a single compromise can cause. What looks like convenience is actually a concentration of risk. This is the same pattern security teams have seen with cloud tools, third-party software, and shadow IT for years."**

**The enterprise security problem is structural.** OpenClaw's popularity drove rapid adoption by individual developers and technical employees, most of whom installed it on personal or work machines without notifying their security teams. The result is a class of high-privilege AI agents operating outside any approved software inventory, security review, or monitoring coverage — precisely the shadow IT risk model that security teams spent the last decade building controls around, now recurring in a context where the "tool" can execute shell commands and access all connected services.

The Reco.ai analysis of the parallel Clawdbot security crisis documents the original vulnerability chain (CVE-2026-25253) that enabled RCE via malicious MCP skill installation. The Adversa.ai hardening guide documents the ongoing exposure for organizations whose developers may be running uncatalogued instances right now.

**Immediate enterprise actions:**
- Run an asset inventory query against your endpoint management platform for OpenClaw/Clawdbot/Moltbot processes or installation directories
- If found: isolate, audit the agent's configuration (what services is it authorized to access?), rotate any credentials stored in its configuration files
- If not found but developers are present: assume instances exist until proven otherwise

### **SLH Is Running a Vishing Recruitment Campaign — $500 to $1,000 Per Call to Impersonate Your Employees**

The threat group Dataminr calls **Scattered LAPSUS$ Hunters (SLH)** — the supergroup formed from LAPSUS$, Scattered Spider, and ShinyHunters — published a recruitment ad on a public Telegram channel on February 22 that is worth reading carefully. The group is actively recruiting women to conduct IT help desk vishing campaigns, offering **$500 to $1,000 upfront per call** with pre-written scripts provided.

This is not a minor operational detail. It is evidence of a deliberate social engineering upgrade cycle targeting a specific gap in enterprise defenses: **most IT help desk impersonation detection relies on voice analysis heuristics and psychological tells developed against male-presenting attackers**. SLH's implicit hypothesis is that a new voice profile will bypass those heuristics — and the structured recruitment via Telegram indicates they've tested this operationally before advertising for scale.

The operational pattern is established from Unit 42's October 2025 Muddled Libra documentation: call the IT help desk, impersonate an employee, request a password reset or remote monitoring tool installation, then use that access to conduct Active Directory enumeration and data exfiltration. In one documented case: Outlook mailbox files and Snowflake database contents, extracted within the same session.

**The relevant defensive upgrade:** Most enterprises have deployed MFA and assume that help desk identity verification is a secondary control. **SLH's playbook treats MFA as the primary target** — they don't try to bypass the token; they social engineer the human who resets it. The fix is **identity verification protocols that don't rely on the caller sounding right** (knowledge-based questions, manager callback procedures, out-of-band confirmation via a second channel the caller cannot control).

The $500-$1,000 pricing tells you something about the economics: these calls succeed often enough to be worth more than commodity phishing, and the recruiter can command a premium for a voice profile they believe increases success rate. That's a signal about your help desk's current defenses, not the attacker's costs.

### **GitHub Trending: What the Security Community Is Building**

**Post-Quantum Cryptography migration tooling is seeing renewed traction** following The Hacker News publishing a practitioner guide on HNDL-aware PQC migration strategy this morning (sourced from Orange Cyberdefense's Security Navigator 2026). The three NIST-approved PQC standards — **ML-KEM (FIPS 203), ML-DSA (FIPS 204), and SLH-DSA (FIPS 205)** — are the target standards for migration, and teams are now building internal capability assessment tools to identify which cryptographic primitives in their stack are RSA/ECC-based and therefore HNDL-vulnerable. The practical timeline from the paper: any data with a confidentiality requirement extending to **2030–2035** is already at risk of harvest-now-decrypt-later capture by patient adversaries.

**StripeApi.Net takedown analysis** is generating supply chain security discussion following the ReversingLabs disclosure. The malicious package's tactics — matching icon, nearly identical README, artificial download inflation (180,000+ faked installs), legitimate project links pointing to real Stripe assets — represent a significant maturation in NuGet typosquatting sophistication. The package was removed before confirmed compromises, but the targeting of Stripe's payment API integration library (74M legitimate downloads) indicates the attacker understood the value of intercepting `StripeService.Initialize()` calls: every downstream payment transaction routed through a compromised install would expose API keys and customer payment data.

**[Claude Code Remote Control](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/remote-control)** launched quietly this week and is the #7 story on Hacker News — Anthropic's new feature allowing Claude to be remotely invoked as a coding agent from external systems. The 308-comment thread is a mix of genuine excitement and security questions about the authentication model for remote access to a code-executing agent. Given the CVE-2026-25802 precedent (model output as XSS vector) and the week's OpenClaw exposure data, the timing of a "remote code execution agent" feature release is generating raised eyebrows from the security community.
