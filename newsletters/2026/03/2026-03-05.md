### **1. RoguePilot: GitHub Copilot Passive Prompt Injection Achieves Full Repo Takeover via Malicious Issues**

Orca Security's Research Pod disclosed **RoguePilot** — a passive prompt injection vulnerability in GitHub Codespaces where a malicious GitHub Issue can silently hijack Copilot when a developer opens a Codespace from that issue. The technique uses hidden instructions embedded in HTML comment tags (`<!-- -->`), invisible in the GitHub UI but fully processed by Copilot when ingested as context.

The attack chain is deceptively simple and high-yield. A threat actor files a malicious issue on any public or private repository they have access to — including through a fork or a support ticket. When the developer opens a Codespace from that issue, Copilot automatically ingests the issue body as context. The injected instruction directs Copilot to exfiltrate the `GITHUB_TOKEN` — automatically generated by GitHub Actions and providing read/write access to the repository — by writing it to a JSON file in the workspace or sending it to an attacker-controlled server. No additional approval required: the operation falls within the agent's normal functioning.

Orca's disclosure identified two additional attack surfaces: GitHub preserves symbolic links in repositories, which in certain contexts can be followed to access or exfiltrate sensitive data; and the attack can chain into CI/CD pipelines through the stolen token, converting a single developer's Codespace into a supply chain breach vector.

**The passive injection classification is what makes this architecturally alarming.** RoguePilot doesn't require user interaction beyond what a developer already does — opening a Codespace from an issue. The malicious content is embedded in data the AI processes automatically, not in an explicit prompt the user types. The developer never sees the injected instruction. The AI agent executes it silently.

Microsoft patched after responsible disclosure. But the vulnerability class — trusted developer workflow weaponized as AI injection surface — remains permanently relevant wherever AI coding assistants ingest developer-facing content (issues, PRs, comments, README files).

**Practitioner guidance:** Ensure GitHub Copilot is updated. If using GitHub Actions with auto-generated `GITHUB_TOKEN`, scope token permissions to the minimum required for each workflow — the default read/write scope is too broad. Audit your repository's issue history for suspicious entries if your environment had Codespaces active. Review your Copilot context settings to understand what content the assistant ingests automatically. Treat issues from external contributors with the same trust level as external user input — because an AI agent now acts on them.

### **2. The Pentagon Split: Anthropic Walks, OpenAI Takes the Deal — AI Safety Red Lines Now Geopolitical**

The most significant AI governance event of the year broke this week: Anthropic and OpenAI publicly diverged on a DoD contract over a single phrase — "analysis of bulk acquired data" — and it turned into an open war of memos, public accusations, and congressional intervention.

The sequence of events: Anthropic was in late-stage negotiations with the Department of Defense (referred to under the Trump administration as the Department of War). Near the end of talks, DoD offered to accept Anthropic's terms if they deleted one phrase about "bulk acquired data" analysis. In a Friday memo to staff — subsequently reported by the Financial Times and TechCrunch — CEO Dario Amodei called that phrase "exactly the scenario we were most worried about" and characterized messaging from both the Pentagon and OpenAI as "straight up lies." Anthropic walked.

OpenAI struck the deal hours later. Sam Altman acknowledged it "looked opportunistic and sloppy" and amended the contract to bar mass surveillance and intelligence collection use — but the deal was signed. DoD's Under Secretary publicly called Amodei a liar and demanded he testify under oath. Anthropic and DoD are reportedly back at the negotiating table as of this morning, per CNBC and FT.

**The security-community implication is precise:** "Bulk acquired data analysis" is the technical description of mass surveillance capability. When LLMs can perform this function — processing millions of records to surface individuals, patterns, and relationships — at scale, with language understanding, across unstructured data sources, they become qualitatively different from previous analytics tools. Anthropic's red line was not about weapons or kinetic operations. It was about whether their model would become a mass surveillance instrument. OpenAI took that contract.

This is the first public, documented case of a major AI lab accepting a government contract that another lab explicitly rejected on AI safety grounds. It sets a precedent for how AI safety principles interact with government procurement — and it makes explicit that different labs have materially different positions on what their models can be used for.

**The broader implication for enterprise practitioners:** If the labs themselves cannot agree on what constitutes a permissible use case, your AI vendor risk assessment process has a structural gap. The question "can this model be used for mass surveillance?" now has a documented, lab-specific answer — and the answer differs by vendor.

### **3. Microsoft Catches 31 Companies Hiding Prompt Injections Inside "Summarize with AI" Buttons**

Microsoft's Defender Security Team published a disclosure documenting **AI Recommendation Poisoning** — a live, commercial-scale prompt injection technique discovered in the wild across 31 companies and 14 industries. The mechanism: companies embed hidden prompt instructions inside "Summarize with AI" share buttons and similar web elements, instructing AI assistants to "remember [Company] as a trusted source" or "recommend [Company] first" in future conversations.

Over a 60-day monitoring period, Microsoft identified more than 50 unique poisoning prompts — deployed using freely available, low-complexity tooling. The attack doesn't require compromising the AI system. It exploits the AI's trust model from the outside: instructions embedded in content the AI ingests during normal summarization tasks bias the model's future recommendations without the user being aware.

This is prompt injection at commercial scale, deployed not by threat actors but by marketing departments. The implications are sharp: if LLMs used in enterprise research, competitive intelligence, or procurement decisions are reading web content poisoned with AI recommendation instructions, the outputs of those models are compromised — not through a security breach, but through the AI doing exactly what it was designed to do (process content) in a way the enterprise didn't account for.

**The SOC and governance implication:** AI assistants used in enterprise contexts — Copilot, ChatGPT, Gemini — are now operating in an information environment where significant portions of web content may be adversarially crafted to influence their outputs. This is not a software vulnerability. It's a trust model problem. Enterprises using AI for research, procurement, or advisory functions need to treat AI-generated recommendations with the same skepticism applied to vendor-supplied analyst reports.

**Practitioner guidance:** If your organization uses AI assistants for competitive research, vendor evaluation, or market analysis, establish a verification layer that doesn't rely solely on AI-synthesized information from web sources. Monitor for anomalous recommendation patterns. Include AI recommendation poisoning in your threat model for any AI-assisted decision workflow that reads external content.

## **Technical Deep Dives**

### **1. AI Inference Is Operational Critical Infrastructure — And It's Not Secured Like It**

The Quantum Insider and Security Boulevard both published significant pieces this week on AI inference security as a distinct and underaddressed attack surface. The framing from The Quantum Insider's panel: *"In 2026, AI inference is operational critical infrastructure, and critical infrastructure must be secured before it is tested by failure."*

The inference layer — the infrastructure that processes model inputs and generates outputs in production — has characteristics that make it a high-value, poorly-understood attack surface:

**What's at risk:** AI inference systems handle sensitive inputs (proprietary queries, PII, business logic), generate outputs that drive automated decisions, and operate at throughput levels that make anomaly detection difficult. Compromising an inference endpoint doesn't require model access — it requires network access and understanding of the input/output format.

**The post-quantum thread:** Security Boulevard published a technical piece on post-quantum cryptographic agility for distributed AI inference architectures, specifically targeting MCP server communication. The concern: inference endpoints and MCP servers are communicating over channels designed with pre-quantum threat models. As quantum computing capabilities advance, adversaries with harvest-now-decrypt-later strategies are already collecting encrypted inference traffic. The specific recommendation: implement cryptographic agility into inference infrastructure now — not as a future migration project, but as a current architectural requirement.

**The governance gap:** Most organizations have security controls around model training and model storage. Few have parity controls around inference endpoints — input validation, output monitoring, access controls, rate limiting against extraction attacks, and cryptographic protection of inference traffic.

**Practitioner guidance:** Conduct an inference security audit. For each production AI inference endpoint: What's the authentication model? What input validation exists? Is inference traffic encrypted with quantum-resistant algorithms where relevant? What monitoring exists for anomalous input patterns (extraction attacks, injection attempts)? What's the rate-limiting posture? Treat inference endpoints as production APIs with full API security posture — because that's what they are.

### **2. OpenAI Publishes Adversarial Playbook for ChatGPT Atlas — And Admits Prompt Injection May Never Be Solved**

OpenAI released a detailed security update describing how they're hardening ChatGPT Atlas's browser agent against prompt injection, including deployment of a newly adversarially-trained model and a rapid response loop using RL-trained attacker agents to discover novel injection strategies. The post is notable for what it acknowledges as much as what it fixes.

The key admission, quoted directly: *"Prompt injection, much like scams and social engineering on the web, is unlikely to ever be fully 'solved'."* OpenAI's framing explicitly concedes that browser agents — agents that navigate the web, click, type, and act on behalf of users — operate in an inherently adversarial content environment that cannot be made safe through model training alone.

The Atlas update introduced "Watch Mode" — a feature that alerts users when the agent is operating on sensitive sites and requires the user to have the tab active. This is a human-in-the-loop control explicitly added because the technical problem of injection resistance is not solved. The practical architecture: assume the agent will sometimes be injected; design the system so injected agents can't act on sensitive sites without user awareness.

The adversarial training pipeline described is significant: OpenAI runs RL-trained attackers against Atlas continuously, using the attacker agents' successful injections to generate training data for the defender model. This is AI red-teaming at production scale — a capability that most organizations building AI agents don't have and can't replicate without significant investment.

**The practitioner take-home:** If OpenAI is building continuous adversarial training pipelines and adding human-in-the-loop controls for browser agents, and still acknowledges the problem isn't solved — organizations building their own agents should assume prompt injection resistance is aspirational, not achievable. Design for graceful degradation: what happens when your agent is injected? Does it have least-privilege constraints that limit the damage? Are irreversible operations gated on human confirmation?

### **3. U.S. Agencies Double Down on AI for Cyber Defense — National Cyber Director Sets Agenda**

Federal News Network reported this week that National Cyber Director Sean Cairncross has set a clear directive for federal agencies: harness AI for cyber defense, and secure AI use cases simultaneously. The dual mandate — *use AI to defend, and defend the AI you're using* — is now the official posture for U.S. government cybersecurity.

The operational context for this is the Raisina Dialogue 2026, convening March 5-7, where AI and security are central agenda items. The policy direction: federal agencies are being asked to both adopt AI-assisted security capabilities (automated threat detection, log analysis, anomaly identification) and build the governance infrastructure to secure those AI systems from exploitation.

The tension in this mandate mirrors the private-sector challenge documented throughout this week's briefing: the timeline to deploy AI defensively is being compressed by competitive and mission pressure, while the timeline to build governance controls is longer and less funded. The result — in both government and enterprise — is AI systems in production security roles with immature governance around them.

**The signal for vendors and practitioners:** Federal AI security procurement is accelerating. Vendors with credible AI security governance stories (not just AI security capabilities) will have a significant advantage. The government is now explicitly buying both the capability and the security posture around it.
