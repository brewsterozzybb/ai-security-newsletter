### **1. Claude Used in Iran Strikes Hours After Trump's Ban — The Governance Contradiction Goes Kinetic**

This is the story of the week, and it lands differently than the Anthropic political fight did.

On February 28, 2026, joint US-Israeli strikes hit Iranian leadership, military, and nuclear-linked sites in what has been described as one of the most significant military operations in years. The Wall Street Journal and The Guardian, citing sources, reported that US Central Command relied on Anthropic's Claude to assist in strike planning — specifically in targeting analysis — during the same hours that the executive order banning Anthropic from federal contracts was being signed.

The contradiction is operational: the directive said "do not use Anthropic tools," and the military that was supposed to comply with that directive was using Anthropic's primary tool in a live kinetic operation. According to reporting from Times of India, the ban was announced *hours* before the operation launched.

**What this reveals:** AI governance directives — including the Anthropic supply chain designation — are running ahead of operational reality. The military had integrated Claude into targeting workflows, and those integrations don't stop working because a procurement directive changes. This is the same gap that's been emerging in enterprise settings: AI gets deployed into critical workflows faster than governance frameworks can track or control it.

**Sam Altman's response:** This morning (March 3), Altman posted an updated internal note to X acknowledging that rushing the announcement of the DoW deal "was a sloppy mistake." The updated agreement now explicitly bans domestic surveillance: "Consistent with applicable laws, including the Fourth Amendment... the AI system shall not be intentionally used for domestic surveillance of U.S. persons and nationals." Intelligence agencies including the NSA are explicitly excluded. The domestic surveillance prohibition is now text, not just intent.

**The practitioner consequence:** If your organization has AI governance directives — use policies, approved tool lists, prohibited integrations — auditing *actual* usage against those policies is a different problem than writing them. The Iran incident shows that operational necessity overrides governance paperwork in practice. AI compliance programs that only document policies and don't verify adherence against actual API call logs are running governance theater.

### **2. US-Israel Strikes on Iran Trigger the Largest Coordinated Cyberattack in History — Retaliation Is Coming**

The kinetic strikes on Iran were accompanied by what multiple security firms are now calling "the largest coordinated cyberattack in history." Iranian internet connectivity dropped to between 1–4% of normal levels by early February 28, according to NetBlocks monitoring cited by Unit 42. Government services, official media, energy infrastructure, and aviation systems were severely disrupted.

The BadeSaba prayer app — with more than 5 million downloads — was compromised to send anti-government notifications calling for "reckoning" against the Iranian regime and promising amnesty to anyone who rises up against government forces, per Wired. It's not confirmed who is behind the app hack. Amazon disclosed an outage at its Middle East data center in the UAE coinciding with the conflict.

**The retaliation picture:** Unit 42's Threat Brief published this morning is the most detailed public assessment available:

- Iranian internet connectivity remains at 1–4%, which is *reducing* near-term sophisticated cyber capability from state actors inside Iran because command-and-control structures are disrupted.
- Iran-aligned threat actors *outside* of Iran — including proxies, hacktivists, and diaspora-linked groups — are the more immediate concern.
- Between February 28 and March 1, more than **150 hacktivist incidents** were recorded across open channels: DDoS, defacements, unverified breach claims across government, banking, aviation, and telecom sectors.
- Active groups: DieNet, Team 313, Liwa Thar Allah, Fad Team, Cyb3rDrag0nzz, and Fynix.
- Cynthia Kaiser (SVP, Halcyon, former FBI cyber executive): "Iran will likely respond in cyberspace. It will probably look like cybercrime and ransomware." The Halcyon intel team is already tracking DDoS botnet HydraC2, hacktivist group Handala, and ransomware group Sicarii.
- Fortune's reporting notes Iran has historically targeted energy, oil and gas, and critical infrastructure — sectors where disruption has outsized geopolitical impact.

**The AI angle:** Unit 42's threat brief notes that Iranian state-aligned groups have previously used AI-enhanced targeted spear-phishing campaigns. With state C2 degraded inside Iran, the most likely near-term threat vector isn't sophisticated AI-enabled attacks — it's hacktivist DDoS and low-sophistication disruptions. But the medium-term Iranian cyber response, once leadership reconstitutes, is a different risk calculation.

**Who should care right now:** Energy sector, aviation, US regional military bases and contractors, Gulf Cooperation Council organizations, and anyone with Middle East cloud infrastructure or operations. Unit 42 is specifically tracking geographic spillover to organizations in countries hosting US military bases.

### **3. CVE-2026-0628 "Glic Jack" — The First High-Severity Agentic Browser Vulnerability, and Why It's a Category**

Palo Alto Networks Unit 42 researcher Gal Weizman disclosed a now-patched high-severity vulnerability in Google Chrome's Gemini Live integration. Tracked as **CVE-2026-0628** (CVSS 8.8) and codenamed "Glic Jack" (short for Gemini Live in Chrome hijack), the flaw allowed malicious browser extensions with only *basic* permissions to escalate privileges and seize control of the embedded Gemini AI panel.

**What an attacker with the vulnerability could do:**
- Access the victim's camera and microphone without permission
- Take screenshots of any website
- Access local files and directories on the system

The attack surface: Chrome's Gemini Live panel uses a `chrome://glic` URL with a WebView component to load the `gemini.google.com` web app. The insufficient policy enforcement in the WebView tag allowed extensions to inject scripts or HTML into a privileged page. Google added Gemini integration to Chrome in September 2025. The flaw was reported November 23, 2025, and patched in Chrome 143.0.7499.192 in early January 2026.

**Why this is a category, not just a bug:**

The Hacker News write-up from Unit 42 makes the critical framing explicit: this is the first documented instance of a CVE arising from baking AI and agentic capabilities directly into the browser. The same capabilities that let Gemini summarize content, translate pages, and execute automated tasks require *privileged access to the browsing environment*. That privileged access is the attack surface. And the attack paths compound:

1. A malicious extension could have exploited the panel directly for privilege escalation.
2. A web page could embed hidden prompts visible to Gemini (prompt injection), instructing the AI assistant to perform actions that would otherwise be blocked — data exfiltration, code execution, lateral navigation.
3. Social engineering + a crafted web page = the victim's embedded AI agent working for the attacker.

Google Gemini is now in Chrome by default. Microsoft's Edge Copilot has similar deep browser integration. Firefox is moving in the same direction. Every browser vendor that ships an embedded AI agent with privileged access is shipping an attack surface that didn't exist two years ago.

**If you run Chrome across your organization:** Verify you're on 143.0.7499.192 or later. Browser auto-update should have handled this, but verify in enterprise fleet management.

## **Technical Deep Dives**

### **1. AI Went from Assistant to Autonomous Actor — and Security Never Caught Up (AIUC-1 Consortium Report)**

The AIUC-1 Consortium, with input from Stanford's Trustworthy AI Research Lab and more than 40 security executives across Confluent, Elastic, UiPath, Deutsche Börse, MIT Sloan, Scale AI, and Databricks, published a substantive briefing today on the security conditions that emerged in 2025 and what practitioners face in 2026.

The numbers are striking:

- **80% of organizations** report risky agent behaviors including unauthorized system access and improper data exposure.
- Only **21% of executives** have complete visibility into agent permissions, tool usage, or data access patterns.
- **64% of companies with annual turnover above $1 billion** have lost more than $1 million to AI failures (EY survey).
- **1 in 5 organizations** reported a breach linked to unauthorized AI use — "shadow AI."
- **63% of employees** who used AI tools in 2025 pasted sensitive company data (source code, customer records) into personal chatbot accounts.
- The average enterprise has approximately **1,200 unofficial AI applications** in use.
- **86% of organizations** have no visibility into their AI data flows.
- Shadow AI breaches cost an average of **$670,000 more** than standard security incidents, driven by delayed detection and difficulty scoping exposure.

The briefing identifies three categories of failure:

**The agent challenge:** AI systems have moved past assistants into autonomous agents that execute multi-step tasks, call external tools, and make decisions without per-action human approval. An agent with overprivileged access and poor containment can cause damage through *ordinary operation* — no attacker required. Databricks Field CISO Omar Khawaja: "AI components change constantly across the supply chain and existing security controls assume static assets, creating blind spots when behavior shifts."

**The visibility challenge:** Shadow AI is now the norm, not the exception. 1,200 unofficial applications means 1,200 potential data exfiltration paths that don't appear in your DLP logs because they're using personal accounts on external services.

**The trust challenge:** Prompt injection from adversarial content — web pages, documents, emails — that reaches AI agents operating with tool access is an unsolved problem. An agent browsing the web on a user's behalf that encounters a malicious page has the ability to be redirected.

### **2. Purpose-Built AI Security Agent Outperforms Baseline by 2.7x on DeFi Vulnerability Detection — and Offensive AI Matches It**

Security research firm Cecuro published benchmark results today that quantify something the community has been debating: whether purpose-built AI security agents actually outperform generic LLM coding agents for vulnerability detection.

The dataset: 90 exploited DeFi smart contracts from September 2024 through early 2026, representing $96.8 million in exploited value (2025 crypto theft hit $3.4 billion total, $1.5 billion from the Bybit hack alone).

The results:
- **Baseline GPT-5.1 coding agent:** 34% vulnerability detection rate
- **Cecuro purpose-built AI security agent (same underlying model):** 92% detection rate
- **Improvement factor:** ~2.7x

The uncomfortable parallel: "Frontier agents now execute end-to-end exploits on 72% of known vulnerable contracts." Offensive capability is advancing in parallel with defensive capability, at roughly similar rates.

Cecuro did not open-source its full security agent "given the risks of making autonomous exploit tooling publicly available." The benchmark dataset and baseline agent are open-sourced.

The implications for the DeFi space extend to any security domain where expert knowledge is in short supply. Human audits of smart contracts are expensive, slow, and point-in-time. Projects that skip them (or can't afford them) now have a viable automated alternative — but that same automation is also available to attackers scanning thousands of contracts at marginal cost.

Mayuresh Dani (Security Research Manager, Qualys TRU): "These findings show that DeFi contract security has now become an 'attacker AI agents vs defenders AI agents' regime for smart contracts."

## **Five Things to Take Into the Week**

1. **AI governance on paper is not AI governance in practice.** The Claude-in-Iran-strikes story is the definitive case study. If you have AI use policies, approved tool lists, or prohibited integrations — start auditing actual API call logs and integration inventories against those policies. The gap between the document and the production system is where your real risk lives.

2. **The Iranian cyber threat is a medium-term event, not a 48-hour event.** Near-term sophisticated attacks from inside Iran are degraded due to connectivity collapse. But Iranian proxies, hacktivists, and geographically dispersed operators are active now. If you operate in energy, aviation, or critical infrastructure in the Middle East, US, Israel, or GCC — elevate your monitoring posture and review your IR plans for ransomware and DDoS scenarios.

3. **Patch Chrome to 143.0.7499.192 or later.** "Glic Jack" is patched, but browser fleet verification matters in enterprise environments. More broadly: any embedded browser AI with privileged access to camera, microphone, files, or screenshots is an attack surface. Build a threat model for it before the next one drops.

4. **Shadow AI is the enterprise security debt you haven't accounted for.** 1,200 unofficial AI applications, 86% of organizations with no visibility into AI data flows, 63% of employees pasting sensitive data into personal chatbots. If your DLP and CASB programs haven't been tuned for AI tool traffic — they're running on a threat model that's two years out of date.

5. **Purpose-built AI security agents work, but so does purpose-built offensive AI.** The Cecuro benchmark is real signal: 92% detection vs. 34% for baseline is not incremental — it's a different class of capability. But the parallel finding — 72% autonomous exploit execution on known vulnerable contracts — means defensive deployment isn't optional. The window in which defenders can use this tooling ahead of widespread offensive adoption is finite. Use it now.
