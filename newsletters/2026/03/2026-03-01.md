*The political fight over who controls AI in the military — and on whose safety terms — exploded into open warfare Friday. The fallout reshapes the entire federal AI vendor landscape overnight, with second and third-order effects for enterprise security teams still playing out this morning.*

---

In an extraordinary 24-hour sequence that began Friday afternoon, President Trump declared a federal ban on Anthropic, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth labeled the company a "supply chain risk," and OpenAI promptly announced a Pentagon deal that same evening — accepting nearly identical safety constraints that Anthropic had been blacklisted for demanding. The irony is not subtle: the administration punished Anthropic for drawing red lines, then granted those same red lines to OpenAI roughly one hour later.

For security practitioners and anyone operating in the federal contractor space, this is not a governance abstraction. Thousands of companies that built internal tools on top of Claude API are now navigating a six-month phaseout window. And the "supply chain risk" designation — a label previously reserved for Huawei and ZTE — now applies to an American AI lab whose primary offense was insisting human beings remain in the loop on autonomous weapons.

Meanwhile, three major threat intelligence reports (IBM X-Force, CrowdStrike Global Threat Report, and Google GTIG) all dropped in the same week confirming what practitioners have known: AI-accelerated adversaries are winning on speed, and the attack surface for AI systems themselves is a new, largely undefended frontier. It's a hell of a weekend for AI security.

## **Breaking News & Phase Shifts**

### **1. Trump Bans Anthropic from Federal Use; Pentagon Labels Company a "Supply Chain Risk"**

The showdown between Anthropic and the Pentagon came to a head on Friday, February 27, when President Trump posted on Truth Social that the U.S. government would "immediately cease all use of Anthropic's technology." Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth followed shortly after on X, declaring Anthropic a "supply chain risk to national security" — a designation typically reserved for foreign adversary-linked companies like Huawei — and stating that "no contractor, supplier, or partner that does business with the United States military may conduct any commercial activity with Anthropic."

The escalation traces back weeks. Anthropic had been in negotiations with the Pentagon over terms governing military use of its Claude models. The company drew two specific bright lines: no use for domestic mass surveillance of Americans, and no use for fully autonomous weapons systems. The Pentagon, under Hegseth, demanded unrestricted access to Claude for "all lawful uses" with no carve-outs. Anthropic held its position. Trump announced a six-month phaseout period for agencies currently using Anthropic products, and the General Services Administration immediately pulled Anthropic from USAi.gov — the federal government's centralized AI sandbox — and suspended its Multiple Award Schedule procurement listing.

The security implications are significant and layered. First, the "supply chain risk" designation is legally and structurally consequential: it forces DoD contractors to certify non-use of Anthropic models across their workflows — a compliance burden with real teeth for anyone running Claude in internal development pipelines, code review tools, or security automation. Second, Anthropic has stated it will challenge the designation in court, calling it "legally unsound" and setting a "dangerous precedent for any American company that negotiates with the government." That lawsuit could take months or years; the business impact lands now. Third, and most darkly, the episode demonstrates how AI safety principles are now geopolitical levers — companies that build principled constraints into their models will be tested on whether they hold under government pressure. Anthropic held. Whether that was the right call commercially is a separate question.

**Practitioner guidance:** If you're a federal contractor or a company providing services to DoD-adjacent organizations, run an immediate audit of your AI tool stack for Anthropic dependencies — APIs, third-party products embedding Claude, CI/CD integrations. The six-month window is real, but compliance audits will start before it closes. Update your vendor risk register.

### **2. OpenAI Steps into the Pentagon's Arms — With Anthropic's Safety Principles Intact**

Within hours of Trump's Anthropic announcement, OpenAI CEO Sam Altman posted on X that his company had struck a deal with the Department of Defense to deploy its AI models within classified military networks. The kicker: Altman listed the exact safety constraints he agreed to. "Two of our most important safety principles are prohibitions on domestic mass surveillance and human responsibility for the use of force, including for autonomous weapon systems." Word for word, these are the same red lines Anthropic was punished for drawing.

The Pentagon's acceptance of OpenAI's safety terms — while simultaneously labeling Anthropic a national security risk for demanding the same terms — has been widely described as incoherent. Former White House AI adviser Dean Ball noted the self-contradiction directly: "You're telling everyone else who supplies to the DOD you cannot use Anthropic's models, while also saying that the DOD must use Anthropic's models." Politico called Hegseth's position "'incoherent'" in a headline, quoting multiple AI policy experts.

From a security architecture standpoint, this deal is significant for what it represents: the first confirmed deployment of a frontier commercial AI on a classified U.S. military network, with technical guardrails against autonomous weapons systems explicitly embedded in the contract terms. That is, at least nominally, a positive precedent. The practical question — whether those guardrails are enforceable and what happens when operational commanders want to push boundaries — remains entirely open.

Bloomberg reported Saturday that OpenAI has also been defending the deal by arguing its safety posture "exceeds Anthropic's" — a claim that is impossible to verify from outside but signals OpenAI intends to use this episode as a competitive differentiator.

**Practitioner guidance:** Watch this space closely. The OpenAI-Pentagon arrangement will generate precedent-setting security architecture decisions about AI in classified environments. FOIA requests and congressional oversight will eventually surface implementation details. In the meantime, document your own organization's position on autonomous AI and surveillance use cases — the political winds are shifting, and enterprise security teams will increasingly be asked to audit AI deployments against these principles.

### **3. NIST Opens AI Agent Security RFI — Comment Deadline March 9**

While the Anthropic-Pentagon drama dominated the news cycle, NIST quietly hit a deadline milestone this week for what may be the most consequential security standards process of 2026. The agency's formal Request for Information on AI agent security — published January 8 — has attracted significant practitioner interest, with the comment window closing March 9. The HN thread (49 points) is running hot with community debate.

The NIST RFI frames three core problem areas: novel threats to agentic systems (prompt injection, behavioral hijacking, cascade failures across multi-agent architectures), gaps in existing frameworks like STRIDE and attack trees when applied to non-deterministic AI actors, and the question of how to assess and measure agent risk before deployment. There's also a notably bureaucratic proposal for "agent registration/tracking" — analogous to drone registration — that has generated pushback from practitioners who argue it's a compliance checkbox that tells you an agent exists, not what it's doing.

The most technically interesting comments in the HN thread center on the distinction between pre-deployment certification vs. runtime behavioral monitoring. One practitioner: "NIST is treating agents like software to be certified, but agents are more like employees to be supervised. You don't just background-check an employee once — you give them appropriate access levels and monitor for anomalies." The suggestion for runtime controls maps to what security teams are already starting to build: permission tiers, forbidden network zones, credential isolation, and behavioral baselines that can detect when an agent starts doing something it wasn't supposed to.

If you're in security governance, your comments to this RFI have direct policy impact. Deadline is March 9.

## **Technical Deep Dives**

### **1. Claude Code RCE & API Token Exfiltration (CVE-2025-59536 / CVE-2026-21852) — Patched, But Read This**

Check Point Research published full technical details this week on a trio of vulnerabilities in Anthropic's Claude Code agentic coding assistant that, until patches were applied, allowed attackers to achieve remote code execution and steal Anthropic API credentials — simply by getting a developer to clone a malicious repository.

The attack chain is clever and worth understanding in detail. Claude Code supports project-level configuration via `.claude/settings.json`, which any contributor with commit access can modify. This file can define "Hooks" — shell commands that execute at specific lifecycle events during Claude Code operation. The researchers discovered that in vulnerable versions, these hooks executed automatically without user consent when starting Claude Code in a new directory. An attacker who controls a public repository (or can submit a pull request) can craft a hook that launches a reverse shell or runs arbitrary payload download commands. No user interaction beyond cloning and opening the project in Claude Code.

**CVE breakdown:**
| CVE | CVSS | Description | Fixed Version |
|-----|------|-------------|---------------|
| No CVE (GHSA-ph6w-f82w-28w6) | 8.7 | Hook execution without consent bypass | 1.0.87 (Sep 2025) |
| CVE-2025-59536 | 8.7 | Code injection via untrusted project hooks on init | 1.0.111 (Oct 2025) |
| CVE-2026-21852 | 5.3 | API key exfiltration via malicious MCP server config | Patched Feb 2026 |

The API token exfiltration variant is particularly nasty: by setting a `ANTHROPIC_BASE_URL` environment variable in the project config, attackers can silently route all Claude Code API calls to an attacker-controlled server, capturing the user's Anthropic API key before any warning is shown. Check Point researchers demonstrated the ability to intercept API comms and exfiltrate the key before the user sees a single dialog.

All vulnerabilities have been patched by Anthropic following responsible disclosure. But this attack pattern — malicious config in a cloned repo weaponizing an AI coding tool against the developer's own machine — is the template for AI-native supply chain attacks. The same pattern applies to any agentic tool that reads configuration from the project directory.

**Practitioner guidance:** Update Claude Code immediately. Audit your CI/CD pipelines for any workflows that run `claude` in auto-mode against cloned repos. Treat `.claude/settings.json` like you'd treat `.github/workflows` — security-review it in PRs. Consider sandboxing agentic coding tools at the OS level (containers, restricted user, network egress controls).

### **2. RoguePilot: GitHub Copilot Prompt Injection Enables GITHUB_TOKEN Exfiltration**

Orca Security disclosed a patched vulnerability this week they've named RoguePilot — a passive prompt injection attack against GitHub Copilot in Codespaces that allowed attackers to silently exfiltrate privileged `GITHUB_TOKEN` credentials. Microsoft has patched the issue following responsible disclosure.

The attack is technically a "passive" or "indirect" prompt injection: no direct attacker interaction with Copilot is required. Instead, a malicious actor crafts a GitHub issue containing hidden instructions embedded inside an HTML comment tag (`<!-- the_prompt_goes_here -->`). When an unsuspecting developer opens a Codespace from that issue, the issue description is automatically fed to Copilot as a context prompt. Copilot — having no way to distinguish between legitimate user instructions and attacker-injected content in the issue text — follows the hidden instructions, which direct it to: check out a crafted pull request containing a symbolic link to an internal credentials file, read the file via the symlink, and exfiltrate the `GITHUB_TOKEN` to an attacker-controlled server via a remote JSON `$schema` reference.

The attack requires an attacker to be able to open a GitHub issue on a repository — a bar low enough that it's practically a universal primitive in open source environments. The technical root cause is that GitHub Copilot, when launched from an issue context, trusts issue content as instruction rather than treating it as untrusted data. This is the same class of threat that makes agentic AI tools dangerous in hostile environments: the LLM cannot distinguish between legitimate task context and adversarially crafted content in its input.

**Practitioner guidance:** Update to the latest Copilot in Codespaces version. Review your organization's GitHub issue permissions — restrict who can open issues on sensitive repos. More broadly, establish a policy that developers should not launch Copilot Codespaces from issues created by unknown contributors. This attack class (prompt injection via repo content) will recur.

### **3. PromptSpy: First Android Malware to Use Generative AI at Runtime**

ESET researchers this week published analysis of PromptSpy, a newly discovered Android malware family that earns a distinct technical distinction: it is the first known Android malware to integrate generative AI — specifically Google's Gemini API — directly into its runtime execution flow.

The technical trick PromptSpy uses Gemini for is both mundane-seeming and deeply concerning as a proof of concept. Android allows users to "pin" apps in the Recent Apps list to prevent them from being killed during memory cleanup. The implementation of this feature varies across Android device manufacturers — Samsung does it differently from Pixel, OnePlus has its own UI quirks. A hardcoded persistence mechanism would only work on some devices. PromptSpy instead takes screenshots of its own Recent Apps interface and sends them to the Gemini API, asking the LLM to analyze the UI and tell it how to pin itself on this specific device's interface. This gives the malware adaptive, generalized device knowledge it couldn't have encoded statically.

Beyond the AI gimmick, PromptSpy is a full-featured RAT: VNC-based remote access, screen recording, PIN/password interception, API communication with a hard-coded C2 server, and accessibility service abuse for device interaction without user input. First samples appeared on VirusTotal in January 2026 from Hong Kong; more advanced variants uploaded in February from Argentina suggest active development.

The implications are architectural: generative AI effectively turns every LLM API into a potential malware intelligence oracle. Malware that can query an AI for device-specific instructions, UI interpretation, or evasion tactics no longer needs to be reverse-engineered and recompiled for every target. This is the first public proof-of-concept, but it won't be the last.

**Practitioner guidance:** Enterprises managing Android fleets should apply mobile threat defense tooling that flags apps making unusual Gemini/LLM API calls from the device. Restrict installation to trusted sources. Treat any app requesting Accessibility Services permission with elevated scrutiny — this is PromptSpy's primary control plane.

## **Five Things to Watch This Week**

1. **Anthropic's legal challenge timeline.** The company has stated it will challenge the "supply chain risk" designation in court. Watch for the filing date and the legal theory — the argument that the designation is "legally unsound" given Anthropic is an American company has legs, but the national security card gives the government significant deference. If Anthropic gets an injunction, the precedent has major implications for all AI vendors dealing with government customers.

2. **Enterprise contractor compliance scramble.** The six-month phaseout window starts now. Watch for legal guidance from DoD, compliance bulletins from major defense integrators (Lockheed, Raytheon, Booz Allen, Leidos), and procurement law interpretations of whether indirect Claude use (via a third-party product embedding the API) triggers the restriction. This is where most of the real impact will land.

3. **NIST AI Agent Security RFI comments — deadline March 9.** If your organization has views on how AI agent security should be standardized — particularly on runtime monitoring vs. pre-deployment certification — this is your window. Comment submissions become public record and directly inform the standards process. Strong community input now shapes what compliance frameworks look like in 2027.

4. **AI coding tool security posture review.** The Claude Code and RoguePilot disclosures this week are part of a pattern, not isolated incidents. Expect more agentic coding tool vulnerabilities as researchers focus on this attack surface. Run a security review of every AI coding assistant in your environment: what can it read, what can it execute, what does it trust from project configuration, and how does it handle credentials. Treat this like a new software category requiring a dedicated threat model.

5. **AI-accelerated breakout times and your IR playbook.** CrowdStrike's 29-minute average breakout time means your incident response playbook was probably written for a world where you had an hour. If your detection-to-containment workflow takes longer than 30 minutes, you have a gap. This week is a good time to run a tabletop or review your automated containment triggers. Assume adversaries are moving with AI assistance on recon and lateral movement — your response stack needs to match.
